## what we're optimizing for

three success vectors. each project lives somewhere on all three, with different weights:

### 1. productization
the headline. *did you ship something useful and get it used.* concretely:
- product-market-fit indicators (not "positive feedback in exploratory interviews" — actual organic pull)
- hockey-stick trajectory or credible path to one
- skill-to-market: distribution readiness, channels identified, partnerships scoped
- *implementing a research IP is not enough.* "no — that has never worked in the past and it will not work for this."

### 2. research grounding & lineage
nice-to-have, differentiator on graduation. the question is whether the architecture is rooted in something real — IC3 / Flashbots papers picked up and pushed forward, or established mechanisms incorporated from outside the team's scope. "it's the right thing to build, built in the right way."

### 3. collaborative contribution
how much have you concretely helped *other* projects in the cohort? this is **retroactively attributed** at graduation — peer rubric, structured interviews, contribution audit. counts toward both individual and project scoring. shared coordination tooling (the commons) is the highest-leverage place to contribute.

## graduation philosophy

graduation is closer to a **class** than a YC demo day. unlike a closed teams-in / teams-out accelerator, the cohort is the unit. individuals are graded on their own trajectory; projects are graded on theirs. **the shape rotation itself is what gets celebrated.**

> "in other words, the individuals will be graded based on what they have done. the projects will be graded by more objective success criteria. as long as you're not failing the class, you're graduating."

what "failing the class" looks like is something we'll define publicly. it is not silent and it is not arbitrary.

## the two peaks

graduation isn't the only checkpoint, and it may not be the loudest one.

- **midterm — ETH NY / Shape Rotator demo night, sun jun 14.** external-facing. this is where the cohort goes public for the first time. by this point most projects can already show significant progress; SWAT-analyses are credible. *optics matter most here.*
- **final demo day #2 + graduation — thu jul 23 or fri jul 24.** more curated, more internal. ~50% of the ceremony is **individual growth + cross-project contribution**, not project demos. for-profit-track projects shift into investor road shows the week prior; commons-track projects shift into grant matching + distribution partnerships.

between the two, expect a meaningful number of pivots, mergers, and project drop-offs. that's the design, not a failure mode.

## per-track outcomes (what "great" looks like)

projects cluster into three rough tracks. the rubric differs per cluster.

### for-profit / commercial viability
- 2-3 projects with credible follow-on funding lined up (not "VC" — the word doesn't mean anything anymore; outside follow-on capital, broadly)
- road-show practice in the last 2 weeks: pitch tuning, curated 3-5 investor meetings per qualifying project, sequenced by likelihood-to-invest
- this is *not* demo day. it's matchmaking with investors who actually have a shot.

### growth / delayed-gratification
- not raise-ready, but on a credible organic-growth trajectory
- next-stage clarity: what you should be looking for in the next 6-12 months, who/what would unblock the biggest growth
- distribution partnerships scoped (which adjacent tools, which channels, which co-distribution paths)

### commons / open-source / coordination tooling
- short-term sustainability: matched with non-profit grants, jumping through grant-writing hoops with help
- distribution at scale: how do you get this in front of every dstack builder / crypto event / agent team / etc.
- 3-5 IC3 research IPs successfully integrated into the right products (so the IP gets *used*, not shelved)

## how we evaluate

multiple feedback loops, none of which is a single-pass "judge ranks all projects."

- **weekly 1:1 office hours.** 1hr/project + 30min/individual. set intention monday, checkpoint friday. did you hit your targets? did you set good targets? *most people set bad targets — that's something to improve.*
- **PMF metrics sharing.** projects identify their own success metrics. cohort gets a (respectful, non-shaming) shared view of how each is tracking against its own bar.
- **retroactive evaluation, starting week 2.** structured rubric for "how has this person contributed to other projects' progress?" peer-based with separate scoring criteria so people can't pattern-match who they're voting for.
- **midterm 360 (anonymized).** after ETH NY demo day, peer evaluation completed over the following weekend. blind-rubric scoring + short structured interviews.
- **AI HR.** small (~5% of evaluation weight) — a safe-space chat for venting + self-reflection. not a leaderboard, not a vote.

## what we deliberately don't measure

- **vanity metrics.** github stars, twitter follower counts, hackathon trophies without context.
- **endorsement counts.** LinkedIn-style "X people endorsed Y for crypto" — interviewing.io data: zero correlation with actual ability.
- **per-person leaderboards.** anyone can see everyone at this scale; rankings turn the cohort competitive when we need it collaborative.

see `program → rules` for the full anti-patterns list.
