# Elocute: AI Speaking-Practice App — Week-2 Project Intro

**A consumer app that bets on adding friction back, not stripping it away.**

*Albiona demoed Elocute, an AI articulation-practice app built around short private speaking sessions, and the room probed monetization, retention, privacy, and agent integrations.*

## the 60-second version

Albiona demoed Elocute, an AI speaking-practice app built around short private articulation sessions, and the room pressure-tested monetization, retention, and privacy. The design bet runs against frictionless consumer apps — private, slightly effortful daily practice as the habit loop — with a TEE-backed private mode floated as the premium tier privacy-conscious users would actually pay for.

## themes

- AI-scored speaking and articulation practice
- Intentional friction as consumer-app design philosophy
- Monetization and retention for consumer AI products
- Privacy and TEEs as a premium differentiator

## insights

- Elocute is a live web app for practicing spoken articulation in two-to-three-minute sessions: users pick a theme and depth level, read a short generated prompt, record themselves speaking about it, and receive an AI score covering delivery (filler words, pacing, structure, language) plus a newer content-accuracy layer that checks whether the substance of what was said actually tracked the topic.
- The product grew out of the presenter's short-form content experiments on high agency and adding friction to daily life; a video about a speaking-club-style two-minute impromptu format drew enough audience demand that an MVP was shipped within days, and traction since has come almost entirely from short-form video rather than any formal launch.
- The core design philosophy runs counter to frictionless consumer apps: the bet is that the most valuable practice is private and low-stakes, so the app aims for a tiny onboarding surface and a daily-consistency habit loop where visible progress (e.g., declining filler-word counts) is the retention hook.
- On the backend the app tracks topic-selection behavior — what users pick, skip, repeat, and favorite — to build a preference graph that personalizes future topic suggestions and stretches users toward weak areas surfaced on their profile.
- The roadmap includes user-uploaded custom topics (pitch scripts, coursework, job-talk prep were all requested), achievement levels and predicted progress roadmaps, shareable challenges, persona-targeted variants (students, non-native professional speakers entering new markets), and longer-term exploration of agent-to-agent communication products under the same communication vertical.
- On monetization the room converged on charging for what actually costs money — compute-heavy depth like custom topics, narrower metrics, and longitudinal tracking — while keeping light features free, with the transition framed as moving users from casually using the product to needing it before adding a paywall.
- Marketing advice from the room centered on selling to the underlying emotional driver (confidence, insecurity, career aspiration) rather than the feature set, since public-speaking improvement is the solution to a felt problem, not the problem itself.
- A retention gap was diagnosed openly: roughly half of users tried the app only a few times, attributed to an inconsistent content cadence and the absence of any callback loop at signup; reminder loops, possibly more personal channels than email, were proposed as the fix.

## q&a

**Q: Can users upload their own material — like a written pitch — to practice against?**

Not yet, but it is among the most-requested features and on the roadmap; people have asked to practice university subjects, job presentations, and pitches against their own content.

**Q: What is the monetization plan?**

First convert users from using the product to needing it, then introduce a paywall; the room's guidance was to gate the compute-costly depth features (custom topics, finer-grained metrics, progress tracking over time) rather than nickel-and-diming basic usage.

**Q: Could this integrate with agents via MCP so practice topics come from a user's own context?**

The presenter was enthusiastic: an MCP integration could pull practice topics from a user's existing LLM conversation threads or product domain, and it was flagged as a near-term build.

**Q: How will privacy be handled when users practice on deeply personal material?**

It depends on the stakes — high-stakes private practice demands clearly communicated data handling, and the room suggested a TEE-backed private mode could be exactly the premium tier privacy-conscious users would pay extra for.

**Q: Why do so many users churn after one to three sessions?**

The presenter attributed it to dropping the content cadence that drove signups and to having no email or callback loop in place at launch; closing that loop with reminders is a priority.
